DESIGN PROCESS

Our Design Process

To create our solution, we began with a comprehensive competitive analysis of existing solutions in the market. After identifying a gap in the market, we conducted academic research to gather information and ensure we had a thorough understanding of the problem we were trying to solve. The design process was an iterative one, involving consultation with a potential user at every step. We designed wireframes on Figma and used them to conduct user research and gathered feedback from a UCI peer counselor, who was an expert in the field of substance misuse recovery Finally, we used all of the information we had gathered to create a high fidelity prototype that fully met the needs of our potential users.

Compettiive Analysis and the Gaps We Found

Existing wearable solutions for substance misuse recovery include the University of Massachusetts’s (academia) E4 Empatica wristband device, which tracks users’ general health through heart rate, motion, skin electrical conductivity, and temperature every 30 seconds. DyAnsys—Drug Relief® is a (industry) device worn behind the ear that sends electricity in the form of pulses to reduce the feeling of addiction, specifically for opioids and especially for early withdrawal. Behaivior is a (industry) wearable that can predict and prevent relapses of substanc e abuse by tracking physical conditions and alerting when relapse is about to happen to allow time to intervene. RFID (radio frequency identification) (industry) technology uses microchips and electromagnetic fields to surveil general patients by tracking their location and used treatments.

Existing website or app solutions include S.A.F.E Alternatives, an online resource website providing a paper control log, videos, interventions, and tips for ending self abuse. The SoberTool app, which is advertised to stop cravings in the moment, includes a flow chart of questions leading to different affirmations that do not change and cannot be edited. The I Am Sober recovery app features daily pledges to be sober, community formation with others in recovery, journal prompts, affirmations, and sobriety streak tracking.

The main gaps are that none of the existing wearable technologies are personally maintained and that the publicly available apps intended for personal use provide only one template for the multitude of users in recovery. Furthermore, the wearables rely on collecting physical information, and many apps currently on the market focus on tracking a user’s sobriety as a whole. Recoup is instead purposed to track the impulses that could lead a user to relapse from their sobriety since emphasizing sobriety tracking, as many existing apps do, could lead users to feel guilt if they do relapse, with the streak loss seeming like a punishment. In comparison, our project is geared towards later stages of recovery and allows the user to have control over their inputs along with other traits such as location and heart rate.

Academic Research

In order to better understand our target demographic and their recovery process, we looked into the recovery process, relapse prevention, and the impact of mindfulness on recovery.

This journal article, Relapse Prevention and the Five Rules of Recovery from YJBM, describes four main ideas: Relapse is a gradual process with distinct stages; this is a process of great developmental milestones and personal growth; main tools are mind-body relaxation and cognitive therapy; and “rules” like being honest with yourself. There are also three stages of relapse. First, individuals experience emotional relapse where they bottle up feelings, isolate themselves, and poor self-care. Mental relapse could be the conflict in the mind between using and not using. Physically would be using the substance again.

The second journal article is Relapse Prevention Strategies & Techniques for Addiction from American Addiction Centers. This journal article describes the neurological background, for example, how misusing substances can change neural pathways surrounding memory, decision-making, and rewards. Feeling triggered to use doesn't necessarily have to be withdrawal symptoms, but maybe also anger, stress, depression, anxiety, peer pressure, fatigue, loneliness, insomnia, relationship issues, sights, and smell. Finally, the way the individual frames the situation has an effect too. If a recovering individual gives into a drink at a party and says they're a failure, it's not good for mental health. Instead, positivity, such as thinking “next time, I'll bring my own beverages to drink” might help.

The article Feasibility, Acceptability and Preliminary Outcomes of a Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention Program… describes an experiment on mindfulness and relapse prevention. They created the Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention program which was an “8-week group program with one 2-hour session per week”, emphasizing “mind, body, and spirit”. At the end of the program, participants reported less alcohol cravings, less anxiety, and less depression. Most of the group also integrated meditation into daily lives or emergencies.

User Research

Rather than personas, we looked into which part of the recovery process our app helps the most, since it was not feasible for us to conduct thorough interviews on anyone in recovery as we were creating the design for our solution. After researching the transtheoretical model of recovery, we determined that our user base would be those who are in stages 3: having determinism to change, 4: taking active change, and 5: maintaining the change.

Survey
We sent a Google Forms survey to UCI class discords, a program manager at the UCI wellness center, and personal contacts as well. We received a lot of feedback from respondents including suggestions that we took into consideration. For example, we scrapped our wearable device idea and that not all of the demographic likes journaling, which we made optional. However, many liked the customizable aspects of the app, and because many individuals tracked their progress, we emphasized our mood tracker and impulse tracking features.

Expert Feedback
We spoke with Cassie Finazzo, a UCI Alcohol & Other Drugs and Peer Education Programs Manager, who shared her knowledge and her own recovery, which gave us many insights to how we could better cater our app. She approved of our concept and Figma wireframes and gave suggestions like flexible notifications and a resources page.
